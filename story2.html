Anxiety and loneliness in Scotland

Where the pressure falls

Mental health is often discussed as if it affects everyone in the same way. In Scotland, anxiety and loneliness are usually presented as shared problems across the population. The reality is more uneven.

Some groups are experiencing much higher levels of strain than others. When broken down by age and gender, clear differences appear. Anxiety is most prevalent among younger adults, particularly women. Loneliness follows a different pattern, appearing at different stages of life depending on gender.

Together, these patterns point to a wider imbalance in how mental wellbeing is experienced. These differences are also linked to wider social inequalities, including income, employment, and area deprivation, which have long been associated with mental health outcomes in Scotland (Scottish Health Survey 2008).

01
Young women and anxiety

The sharpest figure sits with young women

In 2023/24, 33% of women aged 16 to 24 reported experiencing two or more symptoms of anxiety. No other group reaches this level.

Young women stand well above every other category, with a noticeable distance between them and young men. Young men in the same age group also report relatively high levels, at 22%. Early adulthood is clearly a period of pressure, but it is not affecting everyone equally.

Across most working-age groups, women report higher anxiety than men under the age of 65 (Scottish Health Survey 2024). The gap is widest among younger adults and narrows with age, although it does not disappear completely.

The pattern is not completely straightforward. Among older men, 11% of those aged 75+ report two or more symptoms, slightly higher than the 10% recorded among those aged 65 to 74. Anxiety does not simply decline with age, and different groups experience it in different ways.

Taken together, these figures show that anxiety is not evenly distributed across the population. Instead, it is concentrated more heavily among specific groups, particularly younger women.

02
A widening gap over time

The difference has not always been this clear

In 2008/09, both men and women reported the same level of anxiety, at 9%. Since then, the trend has split.

A time-series line chart shows two distinct patterns. Male rates fluctuate moderately over time, but remain relatively stable overall (Scottish Health Survey 2024). Female rates, however, rise more sharply from around 2016/17 onwards (Scottish Health Survey 2024).

They peak at 17% in 2021/22 and remain high at 16% in 2023/24, compared to 11% among men. The gap between the two lines widens steadily across the period.

This pattern reflects a broader trend in Scotland, where women are consistently more likely than men to report indicators of possible psychiatric disorder.

03
Why it matters

Anxiety does not stay contained

Anxiety is something everyone experiences at times, and it can be useful. It helps the brain prepare for stressful situations or potential danger (Mayo Clinic Health System).

However, for some people, anxiety becomes much more intense than the situation requires. In these cases, it can be harmful rather than helpful. Anxiety disorders involve ongoing and overwhelming worry about everyday situations, which can be difficult to control and last for long periods. This can interfere with daily life and lead people to avoid certain places or situations in order to prevent feeling anxious (Mayo Clinic Health System).

People with anxiety disorders are often told that their feelings are not serious or are “just in their head,” which can make things worse by dismissing their experience. While anxiety does begin in the brain, it can also cause real physical symptoms, showing that it affects both mental and physical health (Mayo Clinic Health System).

04
Loneliness tells a different story

Loneliness does not follow the same pattern

It is often associated with older age, but the data points elsewhere. A breakdown by age group shows that the highest levels are not found among the oldest populations. Instead, they appear at different points across the life course.

Among women, 10% of those aged 16 to 24 report feeling lonely most or all of the time. The same group that reports the highest anxiety also reports the highest loneliness, suggesting overlapping pressures in early adulthood.

For men, the peak appears later. 13% of those aged 45 to 54 report frequent loneliness. This mid-life concentration is less visible in public discussion but stands out clearly in the data.

Among those aged 75 and over, levels are lower than might be expected. 4% of men and 6% of women report feeling lonely most or all of the time.

This pattern challenges the idea that loneliness is mainly an issue of ageing. Instead, it shows that it shifts across different stages of life and affects men and women in different ways.

05
A wider pressure

The impact does not stop with individuals

What begins as anxiety or loneliness at a personal level can shape how people move through education, work, and everyday life. Over time, this can affect opportunities, stability, and long-term outcomes.

These experiences are not happening in isolation. Mental wellbeing is closely linked to wider social conditions. People living in more deprived areas, on lower incomes, or outside of employment are more likely to report poorer mental health outcomes (The Scottish Government, 2008). This means that anxiety and loneliness are not only individual struggles but are tied to broader patterns of inequality.

The effects are also becoming visible in public services. Police Scotland now responds to around 14,500 mental health-related call-outs each month, placing increasing pressure on frontline officers. Many of these situations are not criminal but reflect people in distress who have nowhere else to turn.

This creates a knock-on effect. Services that are not designed to provide long-term mental health support are being pulled into that role, highlighting gaps elsewhere in the system.

Taken together, this shows that mental wellbeing is not just a private issue. It is something that shapes, and is shaped by, wider social and institutional pressures.

06
Conclusion

A single general approach is unlikely to be enough

The patterns do not align neatly. Anxiety is most concentrated among young women, while loneliness peaks at different stages of life depending on gender. This shows that mental health pressures are shaped by different factors across the life course.

Support designed for one group may not reach others. A focus on young people may address anxiety among young women, but not loneliness among middle-aged men. At the same time, approaches aimed at older adults risk overlooking younger groups who are also affected.

Mental wellbeing is not evenly distributed. Recognising where pressure falls is essential. Without that, support risks being too broad to reach those who need it most.
