One in three young women in Scotland report anxiety symptoms

Mental wellbeing is not under pressure in the same way for everyone. Anxiety is hitting younger women hardest, while loneliness follows a different pattern across age and gender.

Where the pressure falls

Mental health is often discussed as if it affects everyone in the same way. In Scotland, anxiety and loneliness are usually presented as shared problems across the population. The reality is more uneven.

Some groups are experiencing much higher levels of strain than others. When broken down by age and gender, clear differences appear.

These differences are linked to wider social inequalities including income, employment and deprivation (Scottish Health Survey 2008).

Young women and anxiety

In 2023/24, 33% of women aged 16 to 24 reported anxiety symptoms .

Young men in the same group reported 22%, showing a clear gap.

A widening gap over time

In 2008/09 both genders reported 9% .

Since then, women’s rates have risen more sharply than men’s.

Why it matters

Anxiety can be useful in small amounts, helping people respond to stress (Mayo Clinic) .

However, when severe, it can affect daily life, physical health and decision-making.

Loneliness tells a different story

Among women aged 16–24, 10% report loneliness .

Among men, the highest level appears later in life at 45–54 (13%).

A wider pressure

Mental health is linked to inequality and wider social conditions.

Police Scotland responds to around 14,500 mental health callouts each month .

Conclusion

Mental wellbeing is not evenly distributed. Support must reflect where pressure falls.
