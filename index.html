Housing feature

Edinburgh renters spend nearly double the share of income on housing as those in the Borders

A visually-led feature on the growing affordability gap between Scotland’s capital and lower-cost regions.

1

The pressure is no longer just about high rent

Renters in Edinburgh are now spending nearly a third of their income just to keep a roof over their heads — almost double the burden faced by tenants in other parts of Scotland.

While rising rents have become a nationwide issue, the pressure is far more intense in the capital. The gap between Edinburgh and lower-cost regions such as the Scottish Borders is widening, raising concerns that Scotland is increasingly operating as a two-speed housing market.

2

A market pulling away from the rest of the country

It is not only the size of the gap that matters, but how quickly it has grown. Since 2010, rents in Lothian have more than doubled, rising from around £520 to over £1,000. In comparison, areas such as the Scottish Borders have seen far more moderate increases.

This suggests Edinburgh is not only the most expensive place to rent in Scotland — it is also pulling away from the rest of the country at a much faster rate. For renters, that points to a longer-term structural problem rather than a short-term spike.

3

Why supply matters

One of the key drivers behind rising rents is the sharp decline in available rental properties. Since February 2020, the number of properties available to rent in Edinburgh has fallen from 11,558 to just 4,145. Over the same period, average monthly rent has risen from £1,101 to £1,265.

This combination of falling supply and rising demand has intensified competition for housing, pushing prices higher. The shortage reflects broader issues across the UK, including long-term underbuilding and rising house prices, which have made home ownership more difficult to access.

Disposable income

Higher rents leave less room for savings, transport, food, and everyday spending.

Workforce mobility

Unaffordable city rents can push workers further away from major employment centres.

Policy pressure

Rent controls and supply-side solutions become harder to ignore as the gap widens.

Mental wellbeing feature

Anxiety and loneliness in Scotland reveal stark inequalities across age and gender

A visually structured feature examining who is most affected — and why broad headline statistics do not tell the full story.

1

One in three young women stand out in the data

One in three young women in Scotland report experiencing multiple anxiety symptoms, according to the latest health data, pointing to sharp inequalities in mental wellbeing across age and gender.

Although anxiety and loneliness are often discussed as broad public health issues, the data suggests they are not evenly distributed. Some groups appear to be carrying a much heavier burden than others, challenging common assumptions about who is most affected.

Prevalence of anxiety symptoms by age and gender
Interactive chart
Lead with the strongest demographic contrast so readers can immediately see who stands out.
2

The gap has widened over time

Long-term data from 2008/09 to 2023/24 shows that the gender gap in anxiety has widened over time. In 2008/09, men and women both reported rates of 9%. While male rates fluctuated more moderately over the following years, female rates rose more sharply after 2016/17.

Female anxiety prevalence peaked at 17% in 2021/22 and remained elevated at 16% in 2023/24, compared with 11% among men. This suggests recent increases in anxiety have been concentrated more heavily among women, rather than affecting all groups in the same way.

Why these figures matter

Anxiety and loneliness are not only personal experiences; they are also wider public health concerns. Poor mental wellbeing can affect education, work, relationships, and demand for support services. It can also place pressure on institutions that are often already under strain.

These patterns suggest the need for more targeted responses rather than one-size-fits-all approaches. If vulnerability differs across age and gender, support systems may need to be designed with those differences in mind.
